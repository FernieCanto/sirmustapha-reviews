New Wave. What the hell. There is no such genre as New Wave. Pretty much everything released by the late 70's could be named New Wave if it sounded convenient to the media and the record labels. Talking Heads is quite difficult to put into an specific genre. I guess they fall into Pop, but... what Pop? What do you call Pop with heavy emphasis in clashing rhythm guitars and unusual rhythms, vocal rants aplenty and... God knows what else. If someone comes up with a definition for Talking Heads' music, please let me know.

What matters is, these guys are great. Seriously. They brought into the spotlight an unusual kind of rock music, a kind of music that hadn't been explored very deeply before. These are a nice example of a band that made music the way they wanted to do, thus creating a couple of truly unique albums. There is a certain kind of energy that sparks from albums like More Songs About Buildings And Food and Remain In Light that no other band had managed to gather, and they manage to satisfy the two sides of my soul: the South American, son of Mother Africa, that likes to jerk his shoulders, tap his feet, swing his head and sing along to the music; and the European blooded pseudo-intellectual that likes to search for intelligence and meaning in music. Not that all of their music fits in the "dance" pattern, mind you: they only began sketching such brand of music in their second album, and its culmination would only come with their fourth release, Remain In Light. Meanwhile, they still could come up with rather 'simple', but truly twisted and unpredictable pop music. Armed with rant-vocalist David Byrne, the counterpoint-guitarist Jerry Harrison, and the truly excellent rhythm combo of drummer Chris Franz and bassist Tina Weymouth - Tina particularly gets my thumbs up as a bassist.

For 3 years, the band had Brian Eno as their producer, and he really helped to shape the sound of the band, give it a rich texture and atmosphere, and ultimately bring it to its absolute full potential. Eno's collaboration with band might be one of the most successful collaborations I've ever witnessed, music-wise. The partnership ended when David felt Eno was putting the band in danger of breaking up, so he preferred to faithfully stick to his band rather than let his ego take over. And while David certainly was (and is) a heck of a genius by himself, he wouldn't be able to create the same kind of unique, twisted music without the band. So, thumbs up to our geeky friends. Talking Heads is easily one of my favourite bands coming from the USA - with only The Residents ahead of them, I think.

See here. Talking Heads were weird right from the beginning. They didn't bother with coming up with a flashy album cover, not even with an actual album title. I guess they spent that time writing the actual songs, and if that's the case, I think they've done it the right way. This album is great! You see, this may not be the same Talking Heads you used to hear in the radio all the time, with songs like 'Once In A Lifetime', 'Life During Wartime', 'Burning Down The Housetime' and 'And She Wastime'. This band here sounds like a simple bunch of people that came into a studio, played and recorded all the songs straight in one day, went home, and released the album the next day. Simple, direct, to-the-point and precise. Who needs anything more than that? This is Talking Heads: 77.

And this is really one great album, if only because the songs are so different and unpredictable. They seem like they want to truly do their best of their instruments, and are unwilling to waste even a single second of the record. The songwriting is smart and entertaining, and that sounds enough for me. However, it does sound like the band was just "learning" at that time. Some places sound rather immature and naïve, but that only adds to the fun of the recording! And the "stripped down" nature of the sound only adds to this. You see, the music here sounds more or less like this: two guitars playing almost against each other, drums and bass, Byrne ranting all over, and sometimes an "extra", like a little piano, a little synth, or a little brass. That's all. No overdubs, no wild synth effects, no studio wizardry. Just an extremely tight performance of 11 wacked out, fascinating tunes.

Yes, there isn't one single song in here that I dislike. Even the most "average" ones have a couple of notable things about it, be it Byrne's wild vocal acrobacies, a creative guitar line, a memorable moment, or even a simple detail that jumps out at you with repeated listens. Sometimes, the very meaning of these songs make them outstanding - and I'm not talking strictly about lyrics, or the "intended" meaning. Very few of these songs have a strict meaning - they are suggestive, but they don't have strict meanings. Take 'Don't Worry About The Government', for example. The song is cutesy, bouncy and happy, and Byrne joyfully sings about living in a building. But, isn't there something eerie about the way the sings "I'm a lucky guy to live in my building" or "I'll put down what I'm doing, my friends are important", or even "my building has every convenience"? As if Byrne is immitating that kind of, erm, "brainwashed" happiness that's on the verge of falling apart? It's weird, I know, but it's the impression I get! Maybe Byrne didn't mean exactly that, but it's like I said: these songs are suggestive. You can come up with any meaning you want to come with for these songs, it is your imagination and not mine, after all. And yes, this song is one of my favourites in the album. The music delivers that "frightening happiness" very well.

Sometimes, these oddities aren't really eerie or disturbing, but they're simply downright catchy. The opener 'Uh Oh, Love Comes To Town' stuck to my mind the very first time around. That bassline, the gleeful vocals, the irresistible rhythm, the "OOOH OOOH OOOH OOOOOH OOOOOOH!!" finale... you know! Catchy! Very catchy! Other catchy oddities include 'New Feeling', that features some of the wildest Byrne rants in the record, and some of those strange chord changes that only Talking Heads could make so well. Then, there is 'Who Is It?', a ridiculous little wacked-out groove that doesn't go up to two minutes, irresistible in its dumbness. And there's 'Pulled Up', the song that features the wildest Byrne rants in the record. And there's the most famous song in the album, too - 'Psycho Killer', that really justifies its fame with a catchy bass line, a delightfully goofy performance and 'fa fa fa fa's aplenty.

But really, pretty much every song here is great, in some way. 'First Week / Last Week... Carefree' has a lovely Rhumba-like rhythm and a goofy sax line, 'Happy Day' is a truly lovely, odd ballad, and 'No Compassion' is the band's take on the "two songs in one" approach, and it works. Overall, if you really like the band's songwriting style, you need to get this one. These songs are seeds to many of the band's future glories. It is genuine Talking Heads, only lacking the truly refined Eno production and all. Draw whatever conclusions you want, but I still love this record.

Rating:

Fun factor: 14/15 - Yes, this record entertains me like nothing else can.
Resonance: 11/15 - Well, eh. This is goofy music. Nothing much more.
Originality: 12/15 - Completely un-straightforward straightforward pop.
Richness: 13/15 - There isn't anything really lacking here.
Solidness: 13/15 - The quality never even approaches subpar.

Enter Brian Eno, and the band proceeds to record a groundbreaking, truly original album. Bastards, it's so easy for them. Er, *ahem*, anyway. Yes, this is one of those "genre defining" albums, whatever genre this is defining. Like I said before, I don't consider "New Wave" as a genre, as almost every band defined as "New Wave" is completely different from each other. But yes, this album was sure groundbreaking and original, even though I'm not able to enjoy it as much as its predecessor.

For starters, Eno came in and gave the band a sharp, tight sound, enhancing it in every possible way - and besides that, he provides some smart, subtle synth enhancements and instrument treatments. The two rhythm guitars are the true stars of the show here, though, and in almost every song, they provide the quirky, jerky, contradictory rhythm that's central to the album - and in the meantime, Chris and Tina provide a kickarse, funky rhythm section, and Byrne rants wildly. This album can be seen as the beginning of the "ranting David" era, where the doesn't exactly sing: he mutters, blabbers, yells and shrieks the lyrics instead of delivering an actual melody. The nature of this album led people to call it "paranoid", but I don't know exactly what it is supposed to be. It is never clear what exactly what Byrne is ranting about, but he is ranting, and the music around him is always quirky, jumpy and bouncy. Yeah, perhaps this is "paranoid", but only metaphorically "paranoid". I think.

The biggest positive point about this album is that it is very even, as in, insistently good. Even if the songs are much more similar to each other than they were on 77, there is always something to attract your attention. For example, 'With Our Love' has a truly shattering, sharp guitar work, 'Warning Sign' stars a cool treatment on the drums and a groovy bass line, 'The Girls Want To Be With The Girls' has a delightfully dorky organ popping in all the time, 'Artists Only' is even more dorky and nerdy Byrne vocal acrobatics ("I'm CLEANING, CLEANING again, I'm CLEANING, CLEANING my brain!!"), and 'I'm Not In Love' has a kool little guitar riff. The negative point is that, for some reason, I just fail to find that many catchy elements in the album. 77 had all those infectiously catchy details that make the album truly enjoyable and fun. More Songs About Buildings And Food may sound cool, professional and polished, but it's not not really... fun. These songs are just not very catchy. But that doesn't mean they're not good!

There are few "standouts" in the album, though some of the tunes do sound different from the rest. The opener 'Thank You For Sending Me An Angel' is a hilarious snippet of a march-like groove with Byrne going totally wild. The closer song, 'The Big Contry', is really a slow, lengthy song with some "laid-back" slidey guitar and Byrne reciting his impressive lyrics about not wanting to live in the rural zones of his big country. But I just don't like 'Take Me To The River' very much. I dunno, I guess I don't like that R&B song style after all, and my problems are all against the song itself, not against the band's rendition. It's impressive how the band managed to put their own, unique identity into the song, but I just don't like it, really. It was included here due to influence of their record label, it seems, so the band's forgiven.

Among the other songs - the "meat" of the album - I think 'Found A Job' strikes me all the time. Tina plays a super-fun bass line while David goes talking about people "fighting over little things" and stuff. It's a really irresistible groove, with a fun instrumental coda. 'With Our Love' has its songwriting roots on 'New Feeling', and for me, that's a plus. 'Artists Only' is simply funny, and I love those pinging guitars, the organs and - like I mentioned before - David's vocal acrobatics. 'I'm Not In Love' is fairly catchy, actually, and has some more great instrumental work at the end. Only 'Stay Hungry' never truly caught me, but that's no mean feat. After all, the album brings up truly great ideas, and a unique, priceless sound. If it isn't "fun", well, that won't stop me from checking this out once in a while. It's still a worthwhile buy.

Rating:

Fun factor: 11/15 - Oh, it's good.
Resonance: 9/15 - This album is very... "detached". I guess that's the point.
Originality: 14/15 - Original, it certainly is.
Richness: 12/15 - Cool melodies, cool lines, even though they're way too dependant on the sound and the sound only.
Solidness: 12/15 - Solid, it certainly is. Samey, at times.

Total: 8.4 =

Fear Of Music (1979)

Best song: Memories Can't Wait

Track list:
  1. I Zimbra ++
  2. Mind ++
  3. Paper +
  4. Cities +
  5. Life During Wartime +
  6. Memories Can't Wait ++
  7. Air +
  8. Heaven ++

Yes, my Talking Heads experience began with Remain In Light. But it was only thanks to this album that I was encouraged to go deeper into the band. Man, this is, like... the culmination of the Talking Headism... at least in the intellectual level. You know, as cool as More Songs About Buildings And Food sounded, with the rich production and arrangements, it was just a slick, glossy sphere of coolness. It wasn't really catchy or exactly memorable, nor it seduced you or surround you in atmosphere... Well, this album does exactly that. At least, it did to me. This album, as soon as the first listen, struck me with all its power of seduction, and I was intrigued by it - something that I wasn't for either of the two previous Talking Heads releases. Their debut entertained me, and their second one pleased me very much. But this? This album is addictive as dang.

There might not be the same quantity of jerkiness, quirkiness and gloss as there was on More Songs About Buildings And Food, but here, that jerkiness, quirkiness and gloss are used cleverly, and are interspersed with other cool qualities. This, of course, makes the album less monotonous. We get to see other sides of the Talking Heads music. With the help of Eno's producing skills, the music becomes atmospheric, deep, and - very importantly - intriguing. Unlike the previous albums, there is a sense of meaning in the album that makes me return very often to the album. And this sense of meaning and the atmospheric sound, combined with the excellent music, makes the album a complete package. Much more than Remain In Light, I should say: while this is nowhere near the level of sonic perfection achieved in the 1980 masterpiece, this one is a much more satisfying listen. There is one place here, though, where the band really predicts the whole Remain In Light sound: 'I Zimbra', a really brilliant track. For the first time, we witness the Talking Heads using busy, tribal rhythms with plenty of percussion effects, along with Dadaist lyrics provided by Hugo Ball poem, excited group chants, Tina making a bass groove with the least notes possible, and the fantastic rhythm guitar provided by David and Jerry, along with Eno's pal, Robert Fripp.

In other tracks, we get to see more of those untrivial, unpredictable chord changes. The songwriting here is a true improvement over More Songs About Buildings And Food, and almost all songs here are truly, really catchy. They may be dark, and quite creepy at times, but these tunes pop up in my head all tie time. Each one is very different from the rest, which means that the album never gets boring. You know, what was the last time you heard a Talking Heads ballad, eh? You'll wanna check the gorgeous 'Heaven', that actually doesn't sound like paradise, but is still gorgeous nonetheless. In a less, um, "pleasant" tone, there is 'Mind', with a cool, weird "chuck-a-chuck-a-chuck-a-chuck-a" guitar riff, and David's disturbed moanings. And there's that whacked-out, "cosmic" atmosphere with Eno's synths and all. Very, very cool stuff.

As there was 'I Zimbra' predicting the tribal "World Music" sound, 'War During Lifetime' has an upbeat, funky, brass-laced dance rhythm with some of David's most hard-hitting, realistic lyrics in the record. Yeah, you can guess what it's all about. With titles like those, it's no big deal guessing what the lyrics talk about! Still, what is the meaning of David muttering "hold the paper up to the light! Some rays pass right through!", or rambling about a party in his mind ('Memories Can't Wait'), or talking about Heaven being a place where nothing happens, or going stark raving mad about animals, and concluding by saying that "air can hurt you too"? I don't know, but there seems to be something deep in that - musings of a troubled man about things around him, thinking that everything can harm him somehow - even air. I don't know, but it keeps me hooked. It's a cool album lyricswise, really, and I praise 'War During Lifetime' not only for the words, but also for that funky, catchy tune.

'Cities' is one of the album's most maddeningly addictive songs. David's rantings reach an absolute peak in here, and his performance is enough to make the whole song. Still, there's that electric piano by Eno, that keeps going "Pam, pa-bam. Pam, pa-bam." in the chorus, and more of that funky drumwork and rhythm guitar. 'Animals' is totally mad (well, at least the narrator of the song says he's mad), with guitars hammering out lines that don't really make sense - as if they ever needed to do so - and a hilarious finale. In a less crazy note, the short, catchy 'Paper' has the band at their most, um, "friendly", and the result is another great tune with more, great guitars.

One more song that's up there with my very favourites is the creepy, ominous 'Memories Can't Wait', with some of Eno's very best production jobs with the band. It is slow, dazed and disturbed, but oddly ends in a glorious climax... You know, this might be a quite darned great representation of one stuck in his own mind, as David seems to hint in the lyrics. On the flipside, 'Air' keeps the same "disturbed" climate, but with an even more abstract theme. The music does sound airy, but it's those "aaaaaair" chants that really do it to me. And this time, the ending sounds more on the disturbed side than in the glorious side. Finally, 'Drugs' ends the album making justice to its title. But this isn't the kind of "psychedelic" drugginess, nor the "scary" drugginess. David sounds just weird in here, and his performance is as goofy as on 'Psycho Killer', only more unfocused and augmented by sparse instrumentation that strikes at you are strategic points with blasts of noise and synth - not to mention the weird guitar "solo" at the end.

Many people describe the album as very "creepy", "scary", or whatever. But I'm not so sure as that. David sounds to me as goofy as he was ever been. Perhaps the album sounds "moody", but not really "creepy". At least, I'm not creeped-out by the album. What matters is that this album never loses my attention. Even the feeblest track, 'Electric Guitar' - a slow groove that does nothing in particular - only adds to the general atmosphere. To me, this is the best Talking Heads there can be, and I don't think nothing can change that. This album satisfies me in a way no other album by these guys can do.

Rating:

Fun factor: 15/15 - Well...
Resonance: 15/15 - It's seductive, it's intriguing, and it just won't let go.
Originality: 15/15 - Am I overreacting here? I don't know, but this is one of the most unique records in my collection.
Richness: 15/15 - Yup. Catchy things abound in here.
Solidness: 15/15 - No comments, really.

Remain In Light (1980)

Best song: Born Under Punches

Track list:
  1. Born Under Punches (The Heat Goes On) ++
  2. Crosseyed And Painless ++
  3. The Great Curve ++
  4. Once In A Lifetime +
  5. Houses In Motion
  6. Seen And Not Seen +
  7. Listening Wind +
  8. The Overload

This is the album that everyone in the world hails as the best Talking Heads album ever. Well, of course, this might be the most accomplished Talking Heads album ever. For one, the music reaches an absolute zenith here. It's like band and producer spent months in making the album sound as full, complete, perfect and striking as possible. It's like they took every little aspect of the album, analysed it, and set it to the absolute maximum. Nothing compares to the music in this album, really. As polished as More Songs About Buildings And Food was, and as atmospheric as Fear Of Music was, this is the album that completely reveals the full, true glory of the "Talking Heads sound". And that's pretty much a fact. Liking the record or not, you gotta admit that this was the peak of the mountain for the band as musicians, with Brian Eno firmly in control of production duties.

Well, actually, I'd dare say that Brian Eno is firmly in control of several other things than production duties. He shares songwriting credits with the rest of the band for all compositions, and, with a closer look, it becomes painfully obvious how similar this album is to other Eno-related albums in terms of structures. See? The first half of the album is dedicated to the faster, bouncier and more energetic songs, and the second half completely "mellows out". And this is the same structure Eno used for his own Before And After Science, and David Bowie's Low and Heroes (those two are even more drastically "divided"), and that is definitely no coincidence. And don't forget that, just before this album, Eno and Byrne had released My Life In The Bush Of Ghosts, that, in the words of Eno, is "a laboratory for the Talking Heads album". It was obvious that this album is son of Eno and Byrne (in a purely metaphorical sense - please, take these silly ideas out of your dirty mind!), and it was mostly because of that that David decided to drop Eno.

But anyway, this album. Yes, it's the total culmination of the Talking Heads sound. Problem is, Fear Of Music had a great sound and more. It had a sense of meaning, an atmosphere. It had lots of things behind that sound. Remain In Light is, mostly, sound and sound only. David, at most part, rants and rants, but he rants about something... and it doesn't quite mean anything. 'Once In A Lifetime' is the exception, and a heck of an exception. David's lyrics are hard-hitting in this one, but then, it sounds even more abstract than anything on Fear Of Music. In the other songs, it almost sounds like David simply put together strings of disjointed statements that don't fit together - like in 'Born Under Punches'. Most impressive, though, is that David forces these statements to fit together, and the result is quite amusing. But still, I fail to grab an overall essence like I did on Fear Of Music. Pardon me, I think that was actually the point: be damned if the album doesn't have meaning or "essence". The band was trying to make a statement with this album, and in that, they were successful. They certainly were.

I won't fool you, and I'll say right away that the first half of the album easily outshines the second. Notice that it's almost the opposite of Before And After Science: fans tend to rave about the B side of Eno's masterpiece. But here, the consensus seems to point that side A is the best. And how couldn't it be? You know, the band sure is good when they are "mellow", but this isn't what makes them special. The "mellow" spots in Fear Of Music were much better because they were atmospheric and whacked-out. These four songs aren't (though I should add that an LP I've found at a second-hand store places 'Once In A Lifetime' in side B, not side A! For Chrissake, where's the symmetry?), but I still think they're great. 'Houses In Motion' has David simply moaning over a groovy background, and the backing vocals chanting something catchy in the chorus (I can clearly hear Eno's vocals in there. Mommy, that ugly bald man is scaring me). I also like that solo by a God-knows-what instrument. 'Seen And Not Seen' is better, with a truly gorgeous, dreamy background - great Eno work - and 'Listening Wind' slows the trip down even more, with a slow, spooky and whispery atmosphere to this slightly reggaeish groove. Finally, 'The Overload' makes justice to its title, ending the album in a sombre, sluggish 6-minute drone. But it's good, though, and is quite the opposite of the hopeful, optimistic ending to Before And After Science...

And yeah, that's it. Now, the first half... Well, here lies the gold. Really. From the very first song, the band goes straight into 2nd gear with the bumpy, bouncy, jagged rhythm of 'Born Under Punches', my favourite in the album. David supplies some of his nuttiest vocal pyrotechnics ever, while the whole band goes on making those wacky lines that bounce into each other, going "click-a-click-a, doo-doo-doo-doo, ping-ping-ping, click-a-click-a, boo-doo-boo-doo, beep-beep etc." The rhythm completely makes the song, and as a bonus, you have an infectious "and the heat goes on! and the heat goes on!" chant in the end, and a completely mad synth-like guitar solo - at least, I hear it is Adrian Belew on guitar, making all those noises, but listening to it, I almost refuse to believe. Is that really Adrian Belew? If he is, he should be upgraded to the status of Guitar God immediately.

'Crosseyed And Painless' engages the 4rd gear and speeds through that weird, dissonant guitar line that simply doesn't go away. Great rhythm, great vocal lines, great guitar work, and you know the rest. Plus, it is based entirely in one chord only. Weird, eh? If you're not blown away by now, 'The Great Curve' will blow you away. It engages the 6th gear and speeds up like mad, once again through one chord only. But who cares about the number of chords? If the insanely fast groove doesn't start making your blood boil, go out, have sex, and learn what life is all about - i.e., Dark Side Of The Moon. But anyway, as soon as that symphony of voices blast through your speakers, you'll understand why this album is the musical zenith of the Talking Heads. Adrian Belew plays the guitar solo, and the band chants "WORLD OF LIIIIIGHT, SHE'S GONNA OPEN OUR E-E-EYES UP!" as if they're praising the goddess of Music - Lisa Simpson - or something. It's ultimate coolness. And, of course, who can forget 'Once In A Lifetime'? For the first time, the band makes a song that's fairly friendly for the public - no Psycho Killers quest-ce-que c'est, no life during wartime or anything weird. This one is a heck of a catchy, beautiful and intriguing tune. Those shining synths, the two note bass line, the catchy vocals in the chorus - it ain't exactly what makes the Talking Heads unique, but it works amazingly well. Certainly nobody other than Talking Heads could write something as strange and catchy as this.

But whatever praise I can give to this album, Fear Of Music will always sound better to my ears. I don't think it's yet another case of "personal taste", though - that album is really special to me, "special" in a way Remain In Light can never be. But, if it works as a "consolation", one thing that I can't accuse this album of is being uncatchy. Practically all of these songs are catchy and addictive in some way. It might not be my favourite Talking Heads album ever, but if this album isn't great, I don't know what is.

Rating:

Fun factor: 14/15 - Not a single second bores me, but this album isn't as immaculate as it seems.
Resonance: 14/15 - While this album makes me groove and tap my foot like nothing else, it's not a psychological ride like Fear Of Music.
Originality: 15/15 - No problems here.
Richness: 14/15 - No problems here either.
Solidness: 14/15 - No problems here either. Heck, I told you this was a great album.

Total: 9.6 =

Speaking In Tongues (1983)

Best song: Girlfriend Is Better

Track list:
  1. Burning Down The House ++
  2. Making Flippy Floppy +
  3. Girlfriend Is Better ++
  4. Slippery People +
  5. I Get Wild - Wild Gravity
  6. Swamp
  7. Moon Rocks
  8. Pull Up The Roots
  9. This Must Be The Place (Naïve Melody) ++

The band needed a three year break to go their own ways, do their own stuff, so that they could come back together and make another album. Brian Eno was away already, probably teaming up with Daniel Lanois and Harold Budd and about to become producer for U2. Without him in sight, the band made clear that they didn't want to keep going in the Remain In Light way because... well, from there, there was nowhere to go. They felt that Remain In Light was their peak as musicians, and they just didn't want to try the formula again. Instead, they wanted to have fun. The result was Speaking In Tongues.

From the very beginning, you'll realise this is a stepback - but a counscious stepback, of course. Gone is the impeccable production work, gone is the fantastic guitar and synth work, gone are the multi-multi-layered instruments and voices, gone is the effort to make each song as perfect as possible. These songs are mostly dance ditties written to be accessible, catchy and fun. Of course, rabid fans might have thought it was sacrilege for the Heads to ignore what was best about them. But what's impressive is that, even doing only half of what they were capable of, they managed to pull out something great. This might not be comparable to Fear Of Music or Remain In Light, but it's still a great album to listen to. These dance ditties are accessible, catchy and fun, and for an album of dance ditties, this is alright to me. The songs are pretty simple - twitchy guitars, well-placed synth "embellishments", David Byrne vocal pyrotechnics and catchy - really catchy - lines are the main formula here.

Not that this album is all dance ditties, mind you. 'Slippery People' and 'I Get Wild - Wild Gravity' are two "mellow" songs, and they're good. The former has a great bass line, a clever call-and-response chorus and lots of neat things, and the latter is darker and more mysterious, with a catchy chorus and reggaeish sounds. Rei Momo (1990)

Best song: Don't Want To Be Part Of Your World

Track list:
  1. Independence Day +
  2. Make Believe Mambo +
  3. The Call Of The Wild +
  4. Loco De Amor
  5. The Rose Tattoo
  6. The Dream Police +
  7. Don't Want To Be Part Of Your World ++
  8. Marching Through The Wilderness +
  9. Lie To Me
  10. Office Cowboy ++
  11. Carnival Eyes ++
  12. I Know Sometimes A Man Is Wrong

This is the second LP David Byrne released under his name. Talking Heads was already broken up by this point, and Mr. Frontman was free to fulfill all his solo carreer fantasies. So, this is the next step after Naked: When the last Talking Heads album combined North American songwriting with African and Latin music and instruments, this album goes deep into the Latin American culture and brings out the roots music from the land. This album isn't David Byrne experimenting with the regional music. This album is the regional music: most people playing here are Latin American, some of them share songwriting credits with David in some tracks, there are lyrics in Spanish and Portuguese dialoguing with David's trademark rants, and the rhythms and melodies are pretty much genuine. The most sceptical ones could say that this is only a field experiment, with David studying the local music instead of performing it, and the most cynical ones could even say David pretends to perform it, sticking in the names of the rhythms after the track titles in the sleeve and titling the album "Rei Momo" after a famous folkloric Brazilian personage, thinking it would be enough.

But of course that's not enough. And, no matter how sceptical and cynical I could pretend to be, there's no way I could say those things about the album. If David was really just making "field experiment" with these songs, how come they are so good? It sounds pretty clear to me that David put true effort and energy into the work, and that he cared for it. Possibly he was trying to "study" the music by getting seriously into it, but he was really interested in the culture of the place, not just acting as a poseur. Nah, if he was a poseur, he would have made the project far more grandiose than it turned out to be. And anyway, like I said, these songs are good. Great, in fact. The melodies are rich and warm, the lyrics truly make justice to Byrne's legacy, they are thought-provoking and, overall, fun to listen to. I think you'll just give the record some time, if you really don't feel comfortable with all these Latin rhythms. Even I felt a little bit estranged the first times around, but with just a few listens, I truly realised what was going on, and the songs became filled with hot energy and spark. With songs as fun as these, you have more than enough material to tap your feet and yank your shoulders to, aside from enjoying the vocal melodies and all the nifty work by the backing musicians.

Like I said, each track is indicated with the "genre" of music it follows. And while you may be familiar with words like "Merengue", "Samba" and "Salsa / Reggae", you might frown at strange names like "Orïsa", "Charanga" and "Mapeyé". But that's the least important thing. Overall, each track is pretty much unique. And maybe it's national bias, but the two samba tracks here - 'Don't Want To Be Part Of Your World' and 'Office Cowboy' - are my favourite songs. And even if both are samba, they're still different from each other: the former is a slower, more "rootsy" samba, with a full-blown brass section arrangement and a cuica solo. The song itself is brilliant: the "floating" melody of the verses is countered with the sudden, bright hook of the chorus. The vocals are brilliant to follow, with David truly getting into the "Don't want, don't want, don't want to be part of your world" frenzy, aided by the awesome brass arrangements. 'Office Cowboy', on the other hand, follows the fast, danceable samba of the Carnival as most tourists know it, and the vocal melodies are as great as they could be. David shares the vocal duties with Herbert Vianna, a guy who I'm very fond of, who was, and still is, the frontman of a Brazilian band.

But the non-samba ones are just as worthwhile and good. My favourite among these is 'Carnival Eyes,' that has nothing to do with Brazilian samba really, in spite of its title. But the melody is wonderful. The album actually begins on a rather slow pace, with 'Independence Day'. And while the "energy" level may seem low in the track, it's merely because of the style of playing. It's more laid back, and David goes as far as to bring a fiddle solo, to give a Country taste to the tune. At first, it may seem like the rest of the album is going to bring dirty tricks like those, but that's not true. In many tracks, he's aided by backing vocalists who are very characteristic of Central American music. And they show up on the second track already, 'Make Believe Mambo', that truly sounds like mambo, though it's listed as an "Orïsa". Don't ask me about the difference between the two terms, but you'll recognise the rhythm if you listen to it. Again, great melody, and the lyrics are very Byrnian. Dig the part with the choir going "In my mundo, todo mundo" with David.

Talk about Byrnian lyrics: on a purely lyrical level, 'The Dream Police', with little or no tinkering, could easily fit on one of Talking Heads' classic albums. But here, it's backed by a Cha Cha Cha, which gives it a blissfully sarcastic tone. But the thematics are more abstract and vague - don't want to make social critics and protests in such a fun record, eh? So, you get 'The Call Of The Wild', a poem of true beauty - backed by wonderful music, of course. It's one of the most beautiful tracks, here. 'The Rose Tattoo' has a rhythm reminiscent of 'Mr Jones', from Naked, and Mexican-sounding backing vocalists. 'Lie To Me' is another goodie, using the same Merengue rhythm of 'The Call Of The Wild' for a more fun purpose. Maybe my least favourite here is 'Loco De Amor', but I'm not sure at all why it should be. It's listed as "Salsa / Reggae", but I doubt you'll hear Reggae in it. It's good, too, but a tad more repetitive. But then, you get 'Marching Through The Wilderness', a more laidback tune with a supernifty strings riff, and more of those tasteful melodies.

Overall, it may take a few listens for you to realise it, but these songs are so well written, they truly blow away any feeling that they are just gimmicks. And the performances are energetic, once you understand them. And the project turned out to be a stunning success, in the end. Few artists can penetrate different cultures and churn out works of pure beauty like these. Can you do it? It's not as easy as it seems. You have all sorts of examples of bands trying to do "Eastern" tinged songs, or draw Chinese influences, or whatever kind of influence you may think of. The Cure is there for that. But is it really such a trivial matter to listen to the actual music from the place, care for it, and not just take it for yourself, but try and contribute something to the scene? Honestly, it's a shame that this record is so obscure around here. But I guess people just don't recognise the name "David Byrne", after all. But I can see 'Office Cowboy' being played often on our radios, and what worse, beating many actual Brazilian songs. And the effect would be even better if it was credited as "David Byrne & Herbert Vianna". For your information, Herbert Vianna is extremely famous around here. He even almost died a few years ago.

Rating:

Fun factor: 14/15 - It's exhuberant!
Resonance: 13/15 - I won't pretend that David Byrne truly got the music here, but that would be asking for too much, wouldn't it?
Originality: 13/15 - It would be hard for David being more original than the actual Latin American composers, but he truly knows his business well.
Richness: 14/15 - A ha, THAT's where the gold lies.
Solidness: 13/15 - Perhaps the "meditative" harmonium-driven 'I Know Sometimes A Man Is Wrong' was a stupid move... Nah, that's not it.

Grown Backwards (2004)

Best song: Empire, but I really don't quite know.

Track list:

Ahh, I'll never forget October 9, 2004; the day when I, Fernie Canto, watched Mr. David Byrne play, in my own hometown. It was only the second concert I ever attended, and it left me in such a huge state of euphoria, I don't think I got over it yet. The man was so full of energy, the show was so explosive, the music was so magnificent, everything was just in the perfect measure, I think it's hard to beat that show. I don't think I'd have enjoyed a classic Stones or Who concert more than this one. The atmosphere of the concert was pretty much unbeatable. This man seems enlightened, I don't know. I guess I just like him too much. At this moment, I could say he's my favourite artist, as much as my like Peter Gabriel, Brian Eno, Jean Michel Jarre or that rich, pompous Mike Oldfield who's more full of himself than the world's fattest self-cannibal (though I still like him (Mike Oldfield, not the world's fattest self-cannibal, that is (nested parentheses are great, man)). ... ).

But like I said, David Byrne's show was amazing. And when I got Grown Backwards, I was... a little underwhelmed. Why, you may ask? Well, I don't really know. I think the album was just too relaxed and understated, quite different from the show. It didn't attend my expectations, to be brutally honest. But the problem was that I was expecting the wrong thing. Once I took the album as a relaxed, understated one, I was... erm, the term "blown away" is so overused, but I think it's accurate. This album is quiet, tame and understated, but it's also playful, friendly, subtle, and filled to the brim with melodies and unexpected, diverse instrumentation and arrangements! Yeah, that's right! You can close your eyes and put your finger on any random song out of the 14 presented here, and you can be sure that it has a great, memorable melody, but you'll never know if you'll find the Tosca Strings, a brass section, a samba-like rhythm, Theremins, prepared pianos, tribal-like percussions, a funky drum rhythm or a small orchestra playing an aria from a Bizet or Verdi opera! That's how cool the album is.

Seriously, pretty much every song is a small gem, in a way. And the mood and instrumentation faintly reminds me of Brazilian performers like Caetano Veloso. No doubt he was inspired by them; see, the percussionist is Brazilian! But anyway, if you think Mr. Byrne is only good when being "paranoid", "silly" or "nerdy", I recommend you this one. It's a truly 'human' album, and even the two arias - 'Au Fond du Temple Saint', from Bizet's "Les Pêcheurs de Perles", and 'Un di Felice, Eterea', from Verdi's "La Traviata" - reflect that, somehow. Maybe you could trace some sort of bigger meaning or concept on these songs, what with the album title, but I'm not sure. Me, I just love these two arias, and I'm not even a great fan of Opera, in general. 'Au Fond du Temple Saint' has secondary vocals by Rufus Wainwright, and a load of instruments: strings, woodwinds, harp, euphonium, prepared piano, Theremin, marimba, making quite a sort of "chamber orchestra". 'Un di Felice, Eterea', on the other hand, is gentler, and certainly gorgeous. I was blown away when David Byrne sang this one live. I didn't know he was such a brilliant singer, really, but he is.

Everywhere else, the only song that wasn't written by him is 'The Man Who Loved Beer', and it gains a wonderful arrangement here. And then, there's lots of Byrne for ya. Favourites of mine? Ehhhnn... Man, where do I start? Should I mention the sublime 'Empire' and its fantastic, lush arrangement for horns? Or should I mention the waltzy 'The Other Side Of This Life', or the soft samba 'Tiny Apocalypse'? That's difficult! These songs are all good. Perhaps, David loses it a bit when he gets too dependant on those silly one-note melodies on 'Pirates', but it has a cool beat and fun vocal delivery, which makes it better than John Lennon's 'Julia'. But the soft ballad 'Why' is utterly gorgeous, and the aforementioned 'Empire' is just as much. In a slightly more energetic note, you have 'Dialog Box', and... Argh!

Look, just let me mention the opening 'Glass, Concrete & Stone', and its shivery tribal beat, the mellow cello and marimba and David's dreamy melody. That's a wonderful song! Softer spots are the funny 'She Only Sleeps', the also funny 'Civilization', 'Astronaut' and its great Theremin ensemble, and even the short, silly 'Glad' wins me over. See what I mean when I say all these songs are good? Just listen to them yourself. Of course, you might not like these songs as much as I do if you don't like the style, but why would you dislike the style?

Oh, yes, and there is a bonus track here: The fifteenth track, 'Lazy', which is not "part" of the album, but is inserted here as an addendum. It's a song that David co-wrote with X-Press 2, and this version is based on a remix someone did. So, he uses his trusty back-up musicians, as well as the Tosca Strings, to perform a majorly interesting rendition of the remix. And the song is great, if you ask me! My sister even recognised it when I was listening to it, once. But anyway, Grown Backwards gets a solid 10 from me - if only for the fact that I can't find any faults in it. Yes, a 10. A 10 for you, Mr. David Byrne, and all the cool guys that played with you. And thanks for bringing such a magnificent show to my town, and giving me the opportunity to watch it. I might have handled my money incompetently when I bought the ticket, but it was damn worth it. And it's a shame that the record company owners and businessmen are getting most of the money I paid for your new album, but if you get one cent out of it, I'll be happy.

PS: By the way, should I comment one little thing about the show? His performance of 'And She Was' was excellent! I could finally understand why people like it so much, and I realised one of the reasons why I don't like the album version: it's that stupid, tingly arpeggio that runs all through it! On the show, there was only the fun bass line, and I realised the song it pretty good! I still don't like the album version, though. Same applies to 'Road To Nowhere' - except that I like the album version. But the show version was still better!

Rating:

Fun factor: 15/15 - It's very exhuberant!
Resonance: 15/15 - If you had any doubts that this guy is a human being, listen to this album.
Originality: 15/15 - Well? This is brilliantly-written and brilliantly-performed music, and I can't find anything that smells like "old" here.
Richness: 15/15 - I'm not kidding you, there is nothing lacking here.
Solidness: 14/15 - Hm? Ah, who cares about one single 14 grade, eh?

Total: 9.8 =
